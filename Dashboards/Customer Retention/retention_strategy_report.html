Critical finding: The churn rate has been locked at 91% for every single cohort from 2015 to 2023. No marketing campaign, no pricing change, and no external event (including a global pandemic in 2020) has moved this number even one percentage point.
01 — KEY FINDINGS

What the data tells us

The cohort retention analysis spans 46,913 customers acquired between 2015 and 2023. Every customer whose first purchase was more than 6 months before the dataset's most recent date is eligible for classification. Those with no purchase in the last 6 months are marked churned.

FINDING 01
Churn is perfectly uniform
91% churn appears in every single cohort without exception. 2022 and 2023 nudge to 90% — a single percentage point, not a trend.
FINDING 02
Acquisition has scaled significantly
Cohort size grew from 2,825 in 2015 to 9,010 in 2022 — more than 3× growth. But with retention flat, each new cohort simply creates more churned customers.
FINDING 03
2020 shows a Covid-era dip
The 2020 cohort is the smallest at 3,031 customers — roughly 60% smaller than 2019. Retention still held at 9%, confirming churn is independent of market conditions.
FINDING 04
2022 is the absolute active leader
2022 has the most active customers in raw count (937), but only because the cohort is the biggest — the rate is identical. Volume is masking the retention failure.

03 — ROOT CAUSE HYPOTHESIS

Why 91% persists

A churn rate that is uniform across every cohort, every year, every market condition, and every business size suggests the cause is not external. It is structural. The most likely explanation is a weak or absent post-first-purchase experience — customers buy once, receive no meaningful reason to return, and silently disappear.

Secondary contributors may include: no loyalty programme, no replenishment reminders, no personalised follow-up, and no second-purchase incentive. The business appears to be optimised entirely for acquisition and not at all for retention.

The next analysis step should be computing days between first and last purchase for churned customers. If the majority churned within 30–60 days of acquisition, that confirms the drop-off happens at the very first re-engagement window — and the fix is a post-purchase automation sequence, not a win-back campaign.

04 — RETENTION STRATEGIES

Five actions to break the 91%

01
Map the churn moment — add days_to_churn

Extend the SQL query with last_purchase_date - first_purchase_date AS days_to_churn and plot its distribution. If most customers churn within 30–60 days, the intervention window is right after first purchase — not 6 months later. This single query change will reveal where to focus every other strategy.

Urgent · Do this first
02
Build an automated post-purchase sequence

Implement a 3-touch email sequence for all new customers: a day-7 thank-you with a personalised product recommendation, a day-21 content email (how to get more value from their purchase), and a day-45 incentive (10–15% off a second order). The goal is to manufacture a second purchase — customers who buy twice are dramatically more likely to become long-term buyers. This alone could move retention from 9% to 15–18%.

Urgent · Highest impact
03
Introduce a lightweight loyalty mechanic

The data strongly suggests there is no structural reason for a customer to return. A simple points programme — earn on every purchase, redeem for discounts — creates a pull mechanism that brings customers back without requiring a marketing campaign. Even a "buy 3 get 1 free" stamp card logic would represent a fundamental shift in the retention model. This addresses the root cause rather than chasing symptoms.

Medium-term · Structural fix
04
Tiered win-back campaigns for churned cohorts

With 42,000+ churned customers in the database, a segmented re-engagement programme can deliver immediate revenue. Segment by: recency of last purchase (6–18 months ago vs 2+ years), total lifetime spend, and product category. Customers who spent most and churned most recently should get a personal offer first. Do not run a blanket blast — it will be ignored and damage deliverability. Even reactivating 5% of the 2021–2022 churned base represents 600+ recovered customers at near-zero acquisition cost.

Quick win · Existing database
05
Set a retention KPI and make it visible

The 9% figure has persisted for 8 years partly because it has never been an explicit business target. Set a 12-month goal to move the 2024 cohort retention rate to 15% — a 66% relative improvement that is entirely achievable with strategies 2 and 3 alone. Report this number monthly alongside acquisition metrics. The moment retention is measured and visible to leadership, resources will follow it.

Long-term · Cultural shift
